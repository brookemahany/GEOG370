Describe in your own words how you displayed the map in different projections using QGIS

To change the projection of a map in QGIS, you will want to do click on the bottom right corner of the screen. There will be an ESPG/ESRI code with a small globe icon. This will take you to the project Coordinate Reference System setting. This will change how the data is displayed once you enter the code or name of a new projection into the search bar and click "OK" or "apply".

Mercator Projection

Cylindrical projection which stretches oblongly moving towards the poles; parts of the map are somewhat compressed and stretched horizontally, as well as generally enlarged to almost twice their actual size.

Aitoff Projection

Azimuthal projection which distorts size closer to poles as well as to the far right and left sides of the map. Stretches features near the poles vertically compared to horizontally with Mercator.

Pseudo-Mercator Projection

Cylindrical projection which preserves shape with great distortions in size further from the equator.

Winkel I Projection

Pseudocylindrical projection with vertical distortions away from the equator, though distinct from Aitoff due to being less concave at the poles.

Cylindrical Equal Area Projection

Cylindrical projection which horizontally compresses shape at the poles to a more extreme degree than Mercator.

Equidistant Conic Projection

Conic projection with severe distortion towards the opposite pole from the standard parallel (in this case the South pole).

Azimuthal Equidistant Projection

Azimuthal projection similar to Equidistant Conic in that it stretches horizontally with distance from the pole of focus.

Bonne Projection

Psuedoconical projection which preserves shape at the central meridian and at the pole of focus. Distorts angles and distance. The outline forms the shape of a heart.

Van der Grinten Projection

Polyconic projection with distortion at the poles and curved meridians.
